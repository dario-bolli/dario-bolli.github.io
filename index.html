About me

Welcome to my personal Website!
My name is Dario, I am a recent MSc graduate in Signal Processing and Machine Learning from the Department of Information Technology and Electrical Engineering at ETH Zurich. I am currently working in the Neural Circuits Dysfunction lab at the Champalimaud foundation, where I am responsible for the analysis of neural and behavioural data to uncover biomarkers of dystonia.
Throughout my Bachelor's degree in Electrical Engineering at the Ecole Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne (EPFL), I gained a comprehensive understanding of mathematics, physics, energy conversion and distribution, electronic devices, and information technology. Driven by a strong interest in the field of Artificial Intelligence, I then decided to pursue my academic formation with a Master's degree in Signal Processing and Machine Learning at ETH Zurich. There, I had the opportunity to delve into Computational Neuroscience through a Minor in Neuroinformatics, a dual approach at the intersection between Artificial Intelligence and Computational Neuroscience. Employing Artificial Intelligence tools for the analysis and modeling of neuroscience data facilitates the understanding of neural mechanisms and offers valuable insights into the fundamental principles of biological intelligence. This, in turn, advances the field of Artificial Intelligence, fostering innovation and societal impacts.

My commitment to research and innovation led me to IBM Research's In-memory computing Lab in Zurich, where I worked for six months on brain-inspired solutions to address the ever-increasing need for computational resources in Machine Learning. I then spent a year at the Brain and Behaviour Lab, in the Bioengineering department at Imperial College London, under the expert guidance of Prof. Aldo Faisal . My work there focused on developing computational models of motor-learning mechanisms to address neurodegenerative diseases.
My interest lies at the synergy between Neuroscience and Artificial Intelligence, contributing to neuroscience research, developping neurotechnologies, leveraging insights from neural mechanisms to enhance Artificial Intelligence methods, but also implementing AI solutions to positively impact societal challenges such as improved healthcare, safer transportation, sustainability or faster decision-making.

    • Computational model
    of Motor-Learning

    In this project, we investigate the underlying processes driving sensorimotor learning in real-world settings. To reflect the complexity of real-world scenarios, a pool experiment was introduced. A behavioural analysis of both the pool task and a more conventional motor adaptation task is carried out under two different visual feedback conditions. A thorough review of the literature is conducted, with a focus on learning based on sensory prediction error and reward feedback.

    Our study aims to explore the coexistence of two distinct mechanisms thought to take place in the cerebellum and basal ganglia. Based on the anatomical characteristics of these brain regions, it has been proposed that the cerebellum constructs an internal model of the world to predict the consequences of motor commands. The disparities between predictions and observed outcomes subsequently enable the adjustment of behaviour. Whereas the basal ganglia rely on binary reinforcement feedback to adjust the movement. It has been demonstrated that implicit movement adaptation can be achieved through reliance on either sensory prediction error or reward feedback. When confronted with the multiple degrees of freedom and variables in a realworld task, such as playing pool, opposing trends in neural biomarkers thought to signal the learning related to specific feedback suggest that participants adopt different strategies. The nature of the information provided by these two feedbacks indicates the use of different underlying mechanisms. To further explore this, the real-world pool task was adapted to provide specific feedback on the examination of each underlying mechanism individually. To establish a baseline for our findings, a traditional visuomotor rotation hand-reaching task provided specific visual feedback was conducted in the laboratory.

    This project conducts a comprehensive analysis of the experiments, with a primary focus on the reward condition, as the error condition is already well-established in the literature. Following a thorough review of the motor adaptation field and an analysis of the observed behaviours in the experiments, we defined two simple adaptation models adapted to our experiments. These models make minimal assumptions, are supported by anatomical evidence related to the cerebellum and basal ganglia and include experimentally verified concepts to exploit the information conveyed by the two feedbacks differently. They allow a clear interpretation of the underlying high-level processes and fundamental function of the nervous system and can be readily extended to other motor adaptation tasks. Fitting the parameters of these models to the participants who received error feedback in the reaching and pool tasks showed to be consistent with the prevailing theory of learning based on sensory prediction error using an internal forward model that is updated through Bayesian integration.

    In the context of reward feedback, the model captured the overall adaptation pattern in terms of average behaviour, success rate and variability in both tasks. Additionally, the model exhibited characteristic features of reward-based adaptation, including suboptimality and reward-dependent variability modulation. This confirms that the learner actively regulates variability in his movements based on the performance history to explore the motor space and maximise the reward in a realworld task. In conclusion, the high-level models proposed confirmed several hallmarks of sensorimotor adaptation in a real-world task. They require minimal assumptions and allow for explainability of the underlying processes, providing a solid foundation for further research into sensorimotor adaptation.

    These models present promising avenues for enhancing our comprehension of the functional roles played by the cerebellum and basal ganglia in sensorimotor adaptation. This, in turn, contributes to a better understanding of neurodegenerative diseases affecting these structures, such as cerebellar stroke, cerebellar ataxia, or Parkinson’s disease. Specifically, the real-world pool task setup could be adapted to detect the absence of the reward modulation of movement variability in patients suffering from Parkinson’s disease. Furthermore, personalised therapy could be developed by intelligently manipulating visual feedback for patients to maximise its effectiveness. Finally, this research contributes to a deeper understanding of the overall functioning of the human brain.

    Data-Driven Reinforcement
    Learning to Model Human
    Reward-Based Motor Learning
    in a Real World Task

    In this project, we aim at investigating the neurobehavioural dynamics of sensorimotor learning in real-world settings. We proceed in a first time to a literature review of the relevant research in the field of motor adaptation and reinforcement learning.

    In the second part, we build on top of an existing paradigm to observe learning mechanisms in a real-world task. We employ a data-driven approach to model the reward-based learning mechanism in a visuomotor adaptation task. Specifically, we aim at learning a behaviour policy for the task of playing billiards by observing the actions performed by human participants in a real-world setting harnessing the capabilities of offline reinforcement learning.

    Our Artificial Agent is able to learn a real-world task policy by observing the actions of a human behaviour policy. This framework further allows the analysis of the learning trends when introducing a visuomotor perturbation. Finally, we propose avenues for future research building on this paradigm to enhance our understanding of motor-learning mechanisms.

    Training in Superposition

    Description

    To address the always greedier need of Deep Neural Networks for computational resources, we investigate training and inference in superposition. Exploiting the properties of Hyper-Dimensional Computing, our concept seeks to reduce computation by superposing multiple data examples in a single compressed representation, hence processing the examples simultaneously in the Network.

    We validate the proposed method on image classification on the CIFAR-100 Dataset, using ResNet-12 as a base Classification Network. We first investigate the impact of binding and unbinding operations on the input and output of the ResNet-12. Next, we add a bundling operation to combine the inputs before processing them through the Classification Network.

    Our Results show that the Hyper-Dimensional Computing method does not allow to process multiple images together. Therefore, we modify the initial architecture, and test several data-driven methods derived from this hypothesis.

    We also provide an analysis of the image classification accuracy relative to the number of computational operations saved by means of processing data in superposition. Our best architecture allows to divide by two the number of Multiply-Accumulate operations with a roughly 4.5% reduction in the image classification accuracy when compared with the original ResNet.

    Both the code and the report are confidential.

    Deep-Hedging

    Description

    Hedging is a risk management strategy employed to balance losses in investments by taking an opposite position in a related asset. Mathematical finance provides a tractable solution to hedging in a frictionless and "complete market" models with risk-neutral pricing and hedging. However, trading is subject to transaction costs, permanent market impact, and liquidity constraints in real markets, something not modelled by classical mathematical finance models.

    Deep hedging aims at modeling the trading decisions in the hedging strategies as neural networks. It provides a model-free and efficient solution, enabling us to consider alternative factors such as market frictions. Deep Hedging models' feature sets consist of hedging instruments' prices and can contain additional information such as trading signals, news analytics, or past hedging decisions. We investigated replacing a simple Neural Network by State of the Art Deep Learning Models.

    Deep Learning for
    Autonomous Driving

    Description

    The goal was to do Multi-task learning for semantics and depth estimation on images gathered by 2 cameras mounted on a car, and 3D Object Detection from a Point Cloud gathered by a Lidar, also mounted on the car. The project was split in the 3 following parts:

    1. Understanding Multimodal Driving Data

      That is, creating the visualisation tools for the 3D object detection and point cloud semantic segmentation given a LiDAR point cloud, corresponding RGB camera image, ground truth semantic labels and network bounding box predictions. And processing the Lidar point Cloud, to be able to identify each laser ID from the point cloud directly, and account for the distortion caused by the vehicle motion with the aid of GPS/IMU data.

      If you want to see more, you can have a look at the task, the report, and the code

    2. Multi-task learning for semantics and depth

      The two main tasks in this part are the semantic segmentation (i.e., the task of associating each pixel of an image with a class label, e.g., person, road, car, etc.) and the monocular depth estimation (i.e., the task of estimating the per-pixel depth of a scene from a single image). We worked on the Miniscape dataset. The idea is to train those tasks jointly using Convolutional Neural Networks(CNNs), as the learning of one of the task can be beneficial to solve the other task. We derive an optimal joint architecture based on a shared, separate Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP) and separate Decoders to solve both tasks.

      If you want to see more, you can have a look at the task, the report, and the code

    3. 3D Object Detection from Lidar Point Clouds

      In the last part, we build a 2-stage 3D object detector to detect vehicles in autonomous driving scenes. Our object-detector draws 3D bounding boxes around each vehicle. The First stage, which is often referred to as the Region Proposal Network (RPN), is used to create coarse detection results from the irregular point cloud data. These initial detections are later refined in the second stage network to generate the final predictions. The idea behind such a 2-stage design is to process the entire point cloud in the first stage and to generate regions-of-interest (ROI), i.e. smaller pools of information at and around likely objects which later can be refined individually to form the final detection. In this project, the first stage was prebuilt and pretrained to save time. Therefore, we worked on the initial predictions (i.e. the results of the first stage RPN alongside the extracted features and point cloud data).

      If you want to see more, you can have a look at the task, the report, and the code

    Communication Networks

    Description

    As part of the Communication Networks lecture at ETH Zurich, we, as a group of three, successfully implemented the following two projects:

    1. Routing Project

      The goal of the project is to build and operate an internet-like network infrastructure. Similarly to real networks, our network spans over layer-2 (using switches) and layer-3 (using routers). The network also connects (at layer-3) to other networks using the Open Shortest Path First (OSPF) protocol that provides IP connectivity within an autonomous system (AS). Finally, using the Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) to advertise prefixes between different ASes allow the creation of an internet-like network infrastructure.

      See the assignement and the code

    2. Communication Project

      The task of the second project is to implement a reliable TCP-like transport protocol allowing the communication of binary data over unreliable IP networks such as the internet. The transport protocol is composed of a Go-Back-N sender and receiver.We then implement a Selective Repeat, Selective Acknowledgement and Congestion Control mechanisms.

      See the assignement and the code
